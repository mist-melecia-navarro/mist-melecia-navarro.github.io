MODULE 3:

MOTIVATIONAL INTERVIEWING

"What Do I Say?"

"Don't take all the good lines." Traditional approaches tend to center the practitioner as the advocate for change. It feels like you are pulling out all the tricks to convince the client with arguments for why and how. Module 3 helps answer that question of what is to be said in the therapy session, and in fact, it isn't about what we say but how we draw out the client's own motivations.     

Four Phases of Motivational Interviewing

Motivational Interviewing is a person-centered approach for eliciting (drawing out) behavior change by helping clients to explore and resolve ambivalence.... that feeling of being stuck between yes and no.

1. Engaging

How real is the conversation we are having? Generic? Tailored? I call this the “freebee” step because although there could be some prep, rapport can be built cold. What is your    go to engagement strategy? Me? Small talk before we even get to the “official space”

Tool: OARS, Values ID

2. Focusing

Getting clear direction, the “What?” Without which, it's  just a nice convo. Getting a clear direction of the target behavior. Exploring ⟶ Honing ⟶ Clarifying ⟶ Exploring Ambivalence ⟶ Exploring barriers ⟶ Developing Discrepancy. Avoid the pre-mature focus trap.

Tool: Stages of Change

3. Evoking

Getting into their own internal motivations for change, the “Why?” Reinforce their internal motivations to support the overall motivations for change. Nurture the seed. Look out for “change talk”: Does it sound like they are prepping? Actually mobilizing?

Tool: DARNCAT

4. Planning

Form a plan of action, the “How?” Develop Skills, remove barriers, outside support.

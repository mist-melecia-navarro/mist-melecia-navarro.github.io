MODULE 3:

MOTIVATIONAL INTERVIEWING

"What Do I Say?"

"Don't take all the good lines." Traditional approaches tend to center the practitioner as the advocate for change. It feels like you are pulling out all the tricks to convince the client with arguments for why and how. Module 3 helps answer that question of what is to be said in the therapy session, and in fact, it isn't about what we say but how we draw out the client's own motivations.

Motivational Interviewing Overview

Four Phases of Motivational Interviewing

Motivational Interviewing (MI) is a person-centered approach for eliciting (drawing out) behavior change by helping clients to explore and resolve ambivalence.... that feeling of being stuck between yes and no.

Use the drop downs to see the insider's tips of how to guide conversations with MI.

Engaging Start By Earning a Real Connection

I call this the “freebie” step because, although there could be some prep beforehand, rapport can be built cold. Your job is to make the space feel safe and collaborative. Stay curious! You need enough nuance to understand deeply so really give them that time and space.

Ask yourself: How real is the conversation we are having? Is it generic? Tailored? It might take a while for them to get authentic. Signal that it is okay to be conflicted about things.

What is your go-to engagement strategy? Me? I try to engage in small talk even before getting to the “official space”. If you notice yourself “collecting data,” slow down and come back to the person.

  • Explore values, strengths, hopes.
  • Build collaborative tone (OARS)
  • Avoid “fixing” energy
  • Establish trust and psychological safety.
  • Let their story lead before your agenda

Tools: OARS ( Open-ended Qs · Affirmations · Reflections · Summaries), values/strengths identification

Focusing Leverage the Raw Materials to Forge Ahead

We gotta get a clear direction. We gotta get to the "what." Otherwise, we are just having a nice conversation. To be methodical, move from exploring to honing to clarifying the target behavior.

The comparison between who the client wants to be/says they are with what behaviors they currently engage in, brings this to the surface. This is called developing discrepancy. A taste of the dissonance may bring feelings of shame. Counteract that with cultivating a non-judgemental and empathetic space. Create a space where it is okay to have ambivalence. It's okay to like the "bad stuff" while also considering change.

I give myself the gift of having a home base to go back to: the client's hopes,  dreams, vision for oneself. When the conversations get tougher, the home base adds repreive yet reinforced momentum. With the vision laid out, we have direction, even if the path is rough terrain!

  • Avoid the premature-focus trap (focusing too soon)
  • Develop discrepancy (values vs. current pattern)
  • Agree on a clear direction/ target behavior.
  • Recognize when your client is trying to appease you/your principles

Tool: Stages of Change

Evoking Amplify Their Own Voice So There's No Turning Back

Getting into their own internal motivations for change, the “Why,” helps give us enough substance to build on. It's like building a fire... The ingredients to build a fire might all be present, but without a little nurturing, a flame won't grow as quickly. If you miss the signs, it might just smolder out.

Look out for “change talk”: Does it sound like they are prepping? Actually mobilizing? We want to recognize when we hear them speaking about prepping or beginning to implement change.

Drawing attention to change talk is powerful because it isn't our voice, but their own voice becoming stronger. Sometimes holding a mirror up to someone helps them see something they didnt see before, so use summarizing and reflection techniques.

  • Elicit change talk (DARN-CAT), whether it's about prepping or implementing
  • Amplify client's own reasons
  • Reflect and reinforce intrinsic motivation.
  • Soften sustain talk
  • Roll with resistance, a.k.a. if they start to get scared off don't go into a mode of convincing them.

Tool: DARNCAT statements & prompts (Desire · Ability · Reasons · Need · Commitment · Activation · Taking steps)

Planning Co-create a Plan That Transforms the Energy Into Steps

Transform energy into steps! Form a plan of action! This is the “How?” What is going to get the client ready to move on their goals? Are there skills that need to be developed, barriers to think through, support systems they can rely on?

To gauge if the client is ready for planning, use "the change forumula" as a checklist. DxVxF [>]C. Is there D-Dissatisfaction with the present situation, V- a vision of a better future, and F-clarifty of First next steps for change? And... are all three of these things greater than the perceived Cost of change?

Shrink the gap between wanting and doing by co-create a plan that removes barriers, builds skills, and taps outside supports. Align to Stages of Change for greatest chance of success.

  • Summarize client goals
  • Brainstorm and select strategies
  • Turn motivation into steps.
  • Rehearse skills; remove barriers; line up supports
  • Confirm commitment & follow-up plan

MODULE 1:

THERAPY & THERAPISTS

"What is Therapy, Anyway?"

Research keeps reminding us that you—the person of the therapist—matter a lot. Decades of work show that the early working alliance—shared goals, shared tasks, and a real bond—predicts outcomes across approaches; and even a single session or the first few hours can create durable relief. Clients’ confidence in you and in the treatment plan often moves with their week‑to‑week progress.

Therapist: Know Thyself

Use of Self

First sessions set the tone. When you speak about therapy in clear, human language and show up with steady presence, clients are more likely to feel confident in you and in the plan. We’ll work on your presence, your plain‑language definition of therapy, and building a shared agenda.

The person of the therapist matters. How you show up—your tone, pace, posture, and word choice—shapes the room before the first question.

In social work we call this use of self: the blend of your professional self—training, knowledge, techniques—with your personal self—personality, beliefs, and lived experience.

Dewane (2006) describes five practical channels:

  • Use of personality — the warmth, steadiness, or humor you bring.
  • Use of belief system — holding your values without imposing them.
  • Use of relational dynamics — attunement, empathy, and repair when there’s a miss.
  • Use of anxiety — noticing nerves and letting them inform, not drive, your choices.
  • Use of self-disclosure — sharing sparingly and only when it clearly serves the client.
Therapy: Basic Definitions

Glossary of Terms

Treatment Plan

Roadmap, blueprint, backbone, plan based on assessment and goals/objectives

  • Problems or concerns to be addressed
  • Goals and objectives of the intervention
  • Activities the client will undertake
  • Activities to be performed by the social worker
  • Expected duration of the intervention (in weeks/months)
  • Probable location and number of sessions/meetings
  • Identification of additional people, agencies, or organizations expected to participate and their roles in the change in the process

Assessment

An attempt to understand the client, problem Situation

Purpose:

  • Better understand the client's problems and strengths
  • Guides the development of an intervention or treatment plan
  • Provides foundation from which to monitor progress and the effectiveness of the helping process
